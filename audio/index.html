Module 01 · Part I

Sound as numbers

GoalUnderstand how a continuous sound wave becomes a stream of numbers a chip can process.

Sound is a pressure wave. To store or process it, we measure that pressure many thousands of times per second and write down each measurement as a number — a sample. Play those numbers back through a speaker at the same rate and the wave reappears. Two numbers describe the whole scheme: how often we sample (the sample rate, Module 02) and how precisely we record each value (the bit depth, Module 03).

The shape of the wave is its timbre. A pure sine is a single frequency; a square or saw packs in many harmonics and sounds brighter and buzzier; and noise is all frequencies at once. Every instrument the DSP mixes is ultimately one of these shapes (or a recorded sample) scaled and combined. Play with the scope below — switch shapes and sweep the frequency, and watch the trace.

Key takeaways
  • A digital signal is just amplitude values sampled at regular intervals.
  • Waveform shape = harmonic content = timbre.
  • Two knobs define the format: sample rate and bit depth.

Module 02 · Part I

Sampling & the Nyquist limit

GoalSee why sample rate caps the highest reproducible frequency — and hear what aliasing sounds like.

The Nyquist theorem says you can only faithfully reproduce frequencies up to half the sample rate. Sample at 32 kHz and everything up to 16 kHz is fine — plenty for games. But push a frequency above that limit, or resample carelessly, and it doesn't just disappear: it folds back into the audible range as a false tone. That artefact is aliasing, and avoiding it is a big part of what a good resampler does.

The lab plays the same rising sweep resampled three ways. At 32 kHz (the DSP's native rate) it glides cleanly. Crush it to 8 or 4 kHz with no filtering and you'll hear the highs turn to gravel as they alias — the exact failure the GameCube's polyphase filter (Module 05) exists to prevent.

Sample-rate & aliasingWeb Audio · live
Key takeaways
  • Max reproducible frequency = sample rate ÷ 2 (Nyquist).
  • Frequencies above that fold back as false tones — aliasing.
  • The GameCube DSP runs audio at 32 kHz; streaming disc audio at 48 kHz.

Module 03 · Part I

Quantisation, PCM & ADPCM

GoalLearn how bit depth trades size for fidelity, and how predictive ADPCM squeezes audio into 4 bits.

If sample rate is how often you measure, bit depth is how finely. Storing raw amplitudes is PCM — 16-bit PCM gives 65,536 possible levels and clean sound, but it's big. Round each sample to fewer levels and you save space at the cost of quantisation noise: a gritty crunch as the waveform is forced onto a coarse grid.

The GameCube's ARAM was small, so nearly all its samples are 4-bit ADPCM — a quarter the size of 16-bit PCM. ADPCM is cleverer than plain rounding: instead of storing amplitudes it stores small corrections. Each 4-bit nibble scales a step and adds a prediction built from the two previous samples and a pair of per-block coefficients. Because audio changes smoothly, the prediction is usually close, so 4 bits of correction go a long way.

Drag the bit-depth slider to hear the crunch appear, and compare against the 16-bit reference. Watch the scope trace get blocky as the grid coarsens — that's quantisation, made audible.

On the real chip the accelerator decodes ADPCM as val = scale·nibble + ((0x400 + c1·yn1 + c2·yn2) >> 11), clamped to 16 bits, refreshing the block scale every 16 bytes. You'll meet it again in Module 06.

Key takeaways
  • Bit depth sets the number of amplitude levels; too few = quantisation noise.
  • PCM stores amplitudes; ADPCM stores predicted corrections in 4 bits.
  • ADPCM works because audio is locally predictable from recent samples.

Module 04 · Part I

Mixing, gain & panning

GoalUnderstand how many voices sum into one output, why gains must ramp, and how panning places a sound.

A game plays dozens of sounds at once. Mixing is simply adding their samples together — but with care. Each source has a gain (volume), and if you change a gain instantly you get an audible click, because the waveform jumps. So audio hardware ramps gains smoothly over a few milliseconds instead. Panning places a mono source in the stereo field by sending more of it to one channel than the other.

The mini-mixer below is four synth voices. Set each one's volume (tall slider) and pan (wide slider), mute a few, and play the mix — the gains glide up over 250 ms exactly the way the DSP ramps them. This is the core loop the GameCube's AX ucode runs, just with four voices instead of sixty-four (Module 08).

  • Mixing = summing samples; each voice has an independent gain.
  • Gains must ramp, not jump, or you hear clicks (de-popping).
  • Panning distributes one source across channels to place it in space.

Module 05 · Part I

Filters & resampling

GoalLearn the two DSP tools that shape and re-pitch sound: filters and interpolating resamplers.

The last two fundamentals are filters and resamplers. A low-pass filter removes high frequencies to soften a sound or tame harshness; a biquad is a small, flexible filter that can be a low-pass, high-pass, band-pass or peak. The GameCube gives every voice an optional one-pole low-pass, and Wii voices additionally get a biquad.

Resampling is how one recorded sample plays at many pitches: to play higher, you step through the source faster; to play lower, slower. Since the read position rarely lands exactly on a stored sample, you interpolate between neighbours. Quality is a ladder:

  • Nearest — grab the closest sample. Cheap, but noisy.
  • Linear — draw a straight line between two samples. Better.
  • Polyphase FIR — a multi-tap filter using precomputed coefficients that both interpolates and suppresses aliasing. This is what the DSP uses, with a dedicated coefficient ROM.

Key takeaways
  • Low-pass and biquad filters shape a voice's tone.
  • Resampling changes pitch by stepping through samples at a chosen rate.
  • Better interpolation (polyphase FIR) means less aliasing — hence the coefficient ROM.

The GameCube DSP

Now put the fundamentals into silicon. Nintendo's console routes all audio through one small, programmable DSP. Module 06 · Part II

The audio DSP

GoalMeet the actual chip: its memory, registers, and the accelerator that decodes samples.

GameCube and Wii audio all flows through one custom 16-bit fixed-point DSP made by Macronix. The main CPU never mixes sound itself — it hands work to this chip. It's a Harvard-architecture processor (separate instruction and data memory) with accumulators and address/index/wrap registers tuned for tight signal loops. Nintendo never documented it publicly; almost everything known comes from the reverse-engineering of Duddie & Tratax, still credited in Dolphin's source.

The key block is the accelerator: hardware that streams samples straight out of ARAM and decodes them on the fly. It's where the ADPCM math from Module 03 physically happens — it understands 4-bit ADPCM plus 8- and 16-bit PCM, tracks a loop start/end, and on loop restores the predictor state (pred_scale, yn1, yn2) so the waveform continues seamlessly.

▍

Two boot ROMs. Running the DSP for real (Part III's LLE) needs its IROM and coefficient ROM. Dolphin verifies them by hash — the genuine Nintendo dump is irom 0x66f334fe / drom 0xf3b93527 — and ships a clean-room "free" replacement for people who can't dump their own.

Key takeaways
  • One 16-bit Macronix DSP handles all console audio; the CPU just feeds it.
  • Four small memories: IRAM, IROM, DRAM and a coefficient ROM.
  • The accelerator is dedicated hardware for streaming and decoding ADPCM/PCM.

Module 07 · Part II

One chip, many ucodes

GoalSee how the same DSP is re-tasked by uploadable microcode — for audio, memory cards, even GBA crypto.

The DSP ships blank. At boot it runs a tiny ROM ucode that waits for the CPU to hand it a program — a ucode (microcode) — which it DMAs into IRAM and jumps to. There was never one "GameCube sound engine": each game uploaded its own audio ucode, and the same chip could be re-tasked mid-game to drive the memory card or the GC↔GBA link cable. Coordination happens through two 32-bit mailboxes using a small message protocol (you'll see the handshake in Module 11).

Dolphin's HLE (Module 10) recognises each ucode by a CRC hash of its code. Here are the families, with real hashes and the games behind them, straight from Dolphin's dispatch table.

  • Non-audio ucodes (CARD, GBA) share the very same chip.

Module 08 · Part II

AX: the voice pipeline

GoalTrace one voice through the AX ucode — the exact chain of fundamentals from Part I, in order.

AX is where most GameCube sound lives. There's no fixed voice count in hardware — AX walks a linked list of Parameter Blocks (each PB is a voice) until the chain ends, so the practical ceiling (~64 voices) is a CPU budget, not a silicon limit. AX always runs its math at 32 kHz, in frames of 5 ms × 32 samples on GameCube (3 ms × 96 on Wii).

Every voice runs the same pipeline — and you now know each step:

  1. Fetch & decode — the accelerator streams ADPCM/PCM from ARAM (Modules 03, 06).
  2. Resample — 16.16 fixed-point rate; nearest, linear, or 4-tap polyphase FIR (Module 05).
  3. Envelope — a per-sample volume ramp so gains glide, not click (Module 04).
  4. Filter — optional one-pole low-pass (plus a biquad on Wii) (Module 05).
  5. Mix — summed into MAIN L/R/S and the AUXA/AUXB (and AUXC/Wiimote) buses, each gated by a mix-control bitfield (Module 04).

Output is clamped to 16-bit and written as interleaved big-endian R, L pairs, plus a separate surround buffer carrying the matrix-encoded S channel — which is exactly what the next module is about. Re-open the Module 04 mixer with this list in mind: that's AX in miniature.

Key takeaways
  • AX processes a linked list of voices at a fixed 32 kHz, in millisecond frames.
  • Each voice = decode → resample → ramp → filter → mix, the Part I chain.
  • It writes stereo plus a separate matrix-encoded surround buffer.

Module 09 · Part II

Dolby Pro Logic II — surround from two channels

GoalUnderstand matrix encoding: how four channels fold into a stereo pair, and how a decoder pulls them back out.

The GameCube's audio interface carries only two channels. So how did games do surround? By matrix encoding: fold four channels — left, right, centre and a rear surround — into one stereo pair such that a Dolby Pro Logic II decoder can separate them again. The centre is mixed equally into both channels; the surround is mixed in out of phase and slightly delayed. A decoder recovers the centre from what L and R share, and the surround from what's anti-correlated between them.

On hardware the encoding happens inside the ucode. The Zelda ucode does the most literal version: every voice carries a Dolby position, and a quarter-wave sine table turns that X/Y position into left/right/front/back quadrant volumes (some ucodes even set a "make Dolby louder" flag). AX carries a matrix-encoded surround bus alongside the main stereo. Drag the source around the room below and watch the four channel energies respond; press play to hear the phase-inverted surround cue.

Module 10 · Part III

LLE vs HLE — two philosophies

GoalGrasp the single most important choice in audio emulation: run the chip, or fake it.

Both approaches make sound; they disagree about where the work happens. This is the choice in Dolphin's audio settings.

LLE Low-Level Emulation

Emulate the actual DSP. Load the real ucode into virtual IRAM and execute its instructions one by one.

  • Bit-exact: whatever the game's ucode did, you get.
  • Runs homebrew & obscure ucodes with no special-casing.
  • Enables true Dolby Pro Logic II output.
  • Needs a DSP ROM dump; costs more CPU.
  • Two engines: an interpreter (slow, for debugging) and an x64 recompiler / JIT (the practical choice).
Slower, but the reference for accuracy.

HLE High-Level Emulation

Skip the DSP. Recognise the ucode by a hash of its code and run a hand-written C++ reimplementation of what it does.

  • Fast — no instruction-level emulation at all.
  • No DSP ROM required.
  • Only works for ucodes Dolphin has been taught (AX, AXWii, Zelda…).
  • An unknown ucode falls back to "force AX/AXWii" and may misbehave.
  • Historically lacks full Pro Logic II decoding.
Dolphin's default — "if unsure, select HLE."
Where does the work happen?toggle

LLE: the game ucode is loaded into virtual IRAM. Dolphin's DSP core fetches, decodes and executes each of the ~230 opcodes — arithmetic, the accelerator, the mailbox handshake — exactly as silicon would. The C++ never 'knows' it's mixing audio; it just runs a DSP.

◆

In Dolphin the combo box maps to two flags — DSP HLE and DSP JIT. HLE = HLE on. LLE Recompiler = HLE off, JIT on. LLE Interpreter = both off. Pro Logic II is only offered when HLE is off.

Key takeaways
  • LLE runs the DSP's real instructions; HLE reimplements each ucode in C++.
  • HLE is faster and needs no ROM; LLE is more accurate and enables DPL2.
  • Unknown ucodes are LLE's strength and HLE's weakness.

Module 11 · Part III

Boot, mail & ucode dispatch

GoalFollow the handshake that loads a ucode, and how HLE decides which C++ implementation to run.

At power-on the DSP runs its boot ROM ucode. The CPU sends an init mail (0x8071FEED) followed by a descriptor — the MRAM address, length and target IRAM address of the real ucode. The ROM ucode DMAs that program into IRAM and jumps to it. This is the mechanism behind Module 07: a single game swaps between audio, memory-card and GBA ucodes just by uploading different code.

All coordination rides two 32-bit mailboxes using a small protocol of tagged messages — DSP_INIT, DSP_RESUME, DSP_YIELD, DSP_DONE, DSP_SYNC, DSP_FRAME_END, MAIL_NEW_UCODE. Nintendo's SDK and the homebrew libogc handler share this AX-derived protocol.

Under HLE, an unrecognised hash triggers a panic ("might be incompatible with DSP HLE… try LLE if this is homebrew") and a best-effort fallback to AX. Under LLE there's no lookup at all — it just runs whatever code arrived.

Key takeaways
  • The boot ROM ucode loads the real ucode via a mail handshake + DMA.
  • Two mailboxes carry a tagged message protocol shared with the SDK and libogc.
  • HLE dispatches by hashing the loaded IRAM; LLE just executes it.

Module 12 · Part III

The output path

GoalFollow the samples from the DSP through Dolphin's mixer and out to a real audio backend.

Whatever produced the samples — LLE or HLE — the output lands in Dolphin's Mixer. Two streams merge: DSP/DMA audio at 32 kHz and streaming disc audio (DTK) at 48 kHz. The mixer resamples both to whatever your sound card wants using a windowed, granule-based 6-point Hermite interpolator (the practical cousin of Module 05's polyphase filter), applies volume, dithers back to 16-bit, and hands stereo — or 6-channel float for surround — to the backend.

The backend is whatever your platform provides — Cubeb by default, or OpenAL, ALSA, PulseAudio, WASAPI, OpenSL ES — each simply asking the mixer for the next block of samples. Dolphin can also dump either stream to a WAV file for analysis.

Key takeaways
  • DSP audio (32 kHz) and streaming audio (48 kHz) merge in one mixer.
  • A 6-point Hermite resampler converts to the backend's rate.
  • Optional FreeSurround expands stereo to 5.1; the backend just pulls blocks.

Module 13 · Part III

Try it in Dolphin

GoalTurn the whole course into settings you can toggle and A/B for yourself.

Open Config → Audio and experiment:

  • DSP Emulation Engine → HLE — the fast default. Good for almost everything.
  • → LLE Recompiler — switch here if a game sounds wrong under HLE, if you're running homebrew with an unknown ucode, or if you want Dolby Pro Logic II. Requires a DSP ROM.
  • → LLE Interpreter — for DSP debugging only; very slow.
  • Dolby Pro Logic II decoder — appears only with LLE on a supported backend; pick a quality level to trade CPU for a bigger FreeSurround block.

A good final exercise: boot a Zelda-ucode title (Wind Waker, Twilight Princess) under HLE, then under LLE with DPL2 on, and compare the surround field. The difference is the matrix from Module 09 — the whole course, audible in one A/B.
